FAQ

What is the difference between a 503A and 503B compounding pharmacy?

503A pharmacies compound medications based on individual prescriptions, typically for local patients. They are regulated by state boards of pharmacy. 503B outsourcing facilities can compound larger batches without patient-specific prescriptions and are registered with and inspected by the FDA. 503B pharmacies must follow current good manufacturing practices (cGMP), which provides a higher standard of quality control. For compounded semaglutide, 503B sourcing is the safer choice.

Is compounded semaglutide FDA-approved?

No. The FDA approves specific drug products (like Wegovy), not compounded versions. However, compounded semaglutide is legal when produced by registered 503B outsourcing facilities. The FDA has taken enforcement action against providers using unregistered or non-compliant pharmacies, which underscores the importance of verifying your provider's pharmacy sourcing.

How do I verify that a provider uses a legitimate 503B pharmacy?

Ask the provider directly which pharmacy or pharmacies compound their semaglutide. Then check the FDA's list of registered 503B outsourcing facilities at fda.gov. Legitimate providers should be willing to share this information. If a provider refuses to disclose their pharmacy partner, consider that a red flag.

Can compounded semaglutide have different concentrations?

Yes. Compounded semaglutide is typically available in multiple concentrations, allowing for precise dosing with smaller injection volumes. Your provider should clearly communicate the concentration and injection volume for your specific prescription. Concentration misunderstandings are a common source of dosing errors, so make sure this is clearly documented in your treatment plan.

What happens to compounded semaglutide if the FDA changes its regulations?

The legal basis for compounded semaglutide relates to drug shortage designations and the regulatory framework for 503B pharmacies. If the FDA removes semaglutide from the shortage list or changes enforcement policies, some compounded products could be affected. Reputable providers monitor regulatory developments and will communicate changes to patients proactively.
