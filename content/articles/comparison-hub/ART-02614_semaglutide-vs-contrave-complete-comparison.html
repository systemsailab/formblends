How Semaglutide Works

{{INTERNAL_LINK:Semaglutide}} activates GLP-1 receptors to suppress appetite through your body's natural satiety system. It slows gastric emptying so you feel full longer, reduces hunger signals in the hypothalamus, and improves how your body handles insulin and blood sugar {{CITE:claim}}.

The experience patients describe is a fundamental shift in their relationship with food. The constant background noise of hunger and cravings quiets down. Meals become smaller because satisfaction arrives sooner. Between meals, the urge to snack fades. These changes happen gradually as the dose increases over several weeks.

Semaglutide is administered as a once-weekly subcutaneous injection, with doses titrated from 0.25 mg up to 2.4 mg (for weight management). It is designed for ongoing, long-term use.

How Contrave Works

Contrave takes a completely different approach. It combines two existing medications: naltrexone (an opioid receptor antagonist originally used for addiction treatment) and bupropion (an antidepressant and smoking cessation aid). Together, these drugs target the brain's reward and appetite regulation centers {{CITE:claim}}.

Bupropion stimulates neurons in the hypothalamus that promote satiety and energy expenditure. It also affects dopamine and norepinephrine pathways, which can reduce cravings for food, particularly high-calorie comfort foods. Naltrexone blocks opioid receptors in the brain, which prevents the body from counteracting bupropion's appetite-suppressing effects. It also appears to reduce the pleasurable reward response associated with eating {{CITE:claim}}.

Contrave is taken orally as two tablets twice daily (four tablets total per day at the full dose). The titration period is four weeks, starting with one tablet per day and building up to the full dose.

Efficacy Comparison: Weight Loss Results

Semaglutide significantly outperforms Contrave for weight loss.

The STEP 1 trial showed semaglutide 2.4 mg produced average weight loss of 14.9% over 68 weeks, with about one-third of patients losing 20% or more {{CITE:claim}}.

Contrave's COR (Contrave Obesity Research) trials showed average weight loss of approximately 5-8% of body weight over 56 weeks {{CITE:claim}}. In the COR-I trial, patients on Contrave lost an average of 6.1% of body weight compared to 1.3% for placebo. About 25% of patients achieved 10% or greater weight loss.

While Contrave's results are statistically significant and clinically meaningful for some patients, they pale in comparison to what semaglutide delivers. The gap is roughly threefold: semaglutide produces about three times as much weight loss as Contrave in similar patient populations.

Contrave guidelines suggest that patients who have not lost at least 5% of body weight by week 12 should discontinue the medication, as they are unlikely to be responders {{CITE:claim}}. Semaglutide, on the other hand, continues producing weight loss for 12 to 18 months, with some patients still losing weight past the one-year mark. This extended efficacy window means that patience with semaglutide often pays off, while Contrave's impact is either apparent early or not at all.

Both medications show better results when combined with lifestyle modifications. However, semaglutide's stronger appetite suppression makes it easier for patients to adhere to dietary changes, creating a positive cycle of medication-assisted behavior change that is harder to achieve with Contrave's milder appetite effects.

Side Effects Comparison

The side effect profiles are quite different, reflecting the different mechanisms:

Semaglutide side effects:

  • Nausea (most common, typically improves)
  • Diarrhea and constipation
  • Vomiting
  • Abdominal pain
  • Rare: pancreatitis, gallbladder disease, thyroid tumor warning {{CITE:claim}}

Contrave side effects:

  • Nausea (most common, can be persistent)
  • Constipation
  • Headache
  • Insomnia
  • Dizziness
  • Dry mouth
  • Increased blood pressure and heart rate {{CITE:claim}}
  • Boxed warning: increased risk of suicidal thoughts and behaviors (from bupropion component) {{CITE:claim}}

Contrave's boxed warning about suicidal ideation is significant. Bupropion, as an antidepressant, carries this class-wide FDA warning. While the risk is relatively low, it requires awareness and monitoring. Contrave is also contraindicated in patients with seizure disorders, eating disorders (bulimia or anorexia), uncontrolled hypertension, or concurrent opioid use (since naltrexone blocks opioid receptors) {{CITE:claim}}.

Semaglutide does not carry psychiatric warnings and has a cardiovascular protective profile rather than a cardiovascular risk profile.

The list of contraindications for Contrave is longer than most weight loss medications. Patients taking opioid pain medication cannot use Contrave because the naltrexone component will block opioid effects and can precipitate withdrawal. Patients with a history of seizures, eating disorders, or uncontrolled hypertension are also excluded. Semaglutide's contraindication list is shorter and primarily involves thyroid cancer history and pregnancy.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I take semaglutide and Contrave together?

There is limited data on combining these two medications. They work through different mechanisms, so the combination is theoretically possible, but it is not widely studied or commonly prescribed. Any combination therapy should be supervised by a physician familiar with both drugs.

Does Contrave help with food cravings better than semaglutide?

Contrave specifically targets the brain's reward pathways related to food cravings, which may make it particularly effective for patients who eat in response to emotional triggers or who have strong cravings for specific foods. Semaglutide reduces overall appetite and food interest more broadly. Some patients find one approach more helpful than the other depending on their eating patterns.

Is Contrave addictive?

Contrave itself is not considered addictive. Naltrexone is an opioid blocker (the opposite of an addictive opioid), and bupropion has low abuse potential. However, Contrave should not be used by patients currently taking opioid medications, as naltrexone will block their effects and can precipitate opioid withdrawal {{CITE:claim}}.

Which has a better safety profile?

Semaglutide has a more favorable overall safety profile for most patients. It does not carry psychiatric warnings or cardiovascular stimulant risks. Contrave's boxed warning about suicidal ideation and its tendency to raise blood pressure are concerns that semaglutide does not share. However, semaglutide carries its own warnings about pancreatitis and thyroid tumors that Contrave does not.
