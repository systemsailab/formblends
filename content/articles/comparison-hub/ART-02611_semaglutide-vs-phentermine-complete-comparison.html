How Semaglutide Works

{{INTERNAL_LINK:Semaglutide}} is a GLP-1 receptor agonist that works through the incretin hormone system. It binds to GLP-1 receptors in the brain, gut, and pancreas, producing a coordinated response that reduces appetite, slows gastric emptying, and improves blood sugar regulation {{CITE:claim}}.

The appetite suppression from semaglutide feels qualitatively different from stimulant-based appetite suppressants. Patients describe it less as "not hungry because I am wired" and more as "food just is not as interesting." The reduction in food noise (the constant mental chatter about what to eat next) is one of the most frequently cited benefits.

Semaglutide is administered as a once-weekly injection and is designed for long-term, ongoing use. The medication takes several weeks to build to its full effect as patients titrate through increasing doses. Peak weight loss typically occurs at 12 to 18 months of continuous treatment.

How Phentermine Works

Phentermine is a sympathomimetic amine, meaning it works by stimulating the release of norepinephrine in the brain. This activates the fight-or-flight response at a low level, which suppresses appetite and increases alertness and energy {{CITE:claim}}.

Phentermine was approved by the FDA in 1959, making it one of the oldest weight loss medications still in use. It is taken as a daily oral pill, typically in the morning to avoid insomnia. The appetite-suppressing effect is noticeable almost immediately, often within the first day or two of treatment.

However, phentermine's effects tend to diminish over time as the body develops tolerance. This is one reason the FDA limits its approved use to 12 weeks. Some physicians prescribe it for longer periods off-label, but this practice is debated within the medical community.

As a Schedule IV controlled substance, phentermine carries a low but real potential for dependence and abuse. It is chemically related to amphetamine, though its effects are milder.

Efficacy Comparison: Weight Loss Results

Semaglutide dramatically outperforms phentermine in clinical weight loss data.

In the STEP 1 trial, semaglutide 2.4 mg produced average weight loss of 14.9% of body weight over 68 weeks {{CITE:claim}}. About one-third of participants lost 20% or more. These results were sustained as long as patients continued the medication.

Phentermine studies from over the decades generally show average weight loss of 5-10% of body weight over the short approved treatment period {{CITE:claim}}. A meta-analysis of phentermine trials found an average of about 3.6 kg (roughly 8 lbs) more weight loss than placebo over the study periods {{CITE:claim}}. Results are meaningful but modest compared to semaglutide.

The difference becomes even more pronounced when you consider durability. Semaglutide's weight loss continues to build over a year or more of use, while phentermine's effectiveness plateaus quickly due to tolerance. Most patients who stop phentermine after the 12-week approved course regain the weight they lost.

The patient experience also differs in meaningful ways. Phentermine suppresses appetite through stimulation, which can feel like a wired, energized state where food is not on your mind because you are buzzing with nervous energy. Semaglutide suppresses appetite through hormonal signaling, which patients describe as simply not being hungry and not thinking about food. For most patients, the hormonal approach feels more natural and sustainable over time.

There is also the question of metabolic health beyond the scale. Semaglutide improves blood sugar, A1C, blood pressure, and cholesterol levels. It has been shown to reduce cardiovascular events in overweight patients {{CITE:claim}}. Phentermine, by contrast, raises blood pressure and heart rate, which are the opposite of what most overweight patients need. The metabolic benefits of semaglutide extend well beyond weight loss alone.

Side Effects Comparison

The side effect profiles reflect the fundamentally different mechanisms of these drugs.

Semaglutide side effects (primarily GI):

  • Nausea (most common, usually improves over time)
  • Diarrhea and constipation
  • Vomiting
  • Abdominal pain
  • Headache
  • Rare: pancreatitis, gallbladder disease, thyroid tumors (animal studies) {{CITE:claim}}

Phentermine side effects (primarily stimulant-related):

  • Insomnia (very common)
  • Dry mouth
  • Increased heart rate and blood pressure {{CITE:claim}}
  • Restlessness, anxiety, irritability
  • Constipation
  • Rare: heart palpitations, pulmonary hypertension (historically linked to fen-phen) {{CITE:claim}}

Phentermine is contraindicated in patients with cardiovascular disease, uncontrolled hypertension, hyperthyroidism, glaucoma, and a history of substance abuse. Its stimulant nature makes it inappropriate for many patients who would be good candidates for semaglutide.

Semaglutide's side effects, while uncomfortable, are generally non-cardiovascular and tend to improve with time and proper titration.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I take semaglutide and phentermine together?

Some physicians do prescribe both concurrently, though this is an off-label practice. The combination addresses appetite through two different pathways and may enhance weight loss for some patients. This should only be done under close medical supervision.

Why not just use phentermine since it is so much cheaper?

Phentermine produces less weight loss, is only approved for short-term use, causes stimulant side effects, and leads to weight regain after discontinuation in most patients. While it is cheaper per month, the long-term value proposition favors semaglutide for patients who need meaningful, sustained weight loss.

Is phentermine safer than semaglutide?

Not necessarily. Phentermine's stimulant effects raise blood pressure and heart rate, making it risky for patients with cardiovascular disease. Semaglutide, by contrast, has shown cardiovascular protective effects. Each drug has its own risk profile, and the "safer" option depends on the individual patient's health status.

How quickly does each medication start working?

Phentermine suppresses appetite within the first day or two. Semaglutide takes longer because of the gradual titration schedule, with full effects developing over weeks to months. However, many semaglutide patients notice reduced appetite within the first week of treatment.
