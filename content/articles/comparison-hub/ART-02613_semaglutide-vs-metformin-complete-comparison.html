How Semaglutide Works

Semaglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist that mimics the incretin hormone GLP-1. By activating GLP-1 receptors, it produces multiple metabolic effects: suppressed appetite, delayed gastric emptying, increased insulin secretion (glucose-dependent), and reduced glucagon release .

For weight loss, the appetite suppression and delayed stomach emptying are the most impactful effects. Patients eat less naturally because they feel full sooner and stay full longer. The hormonal nature of this appetite control distinguishes it from stimulant-based appetite suppressants. It works with your body's own satiety system rather than overpowering it with a stimulant.

Injectable semaglutide is available as Ozempic (for diabetes) and Wegovy (for weight management). An oral form, Rybelsus, is available for diabetes but produces less weight loss than the injectable version.

How Metformin Works

Metformin is a biguanide that works primarily by reducing glucose production in the liver and improving insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues . Unlike semaglutide, metformin does not directly suppress appetite or slow gastric emptying, though some patients do experience reduced appetite as a secondary effect.

Metformin's weight loss effect is modest and likely results from a combination of factors: reduced insulin levels (high insulin promotes fat storage), mild appetite suppression in some patients, and possible effects on gut bacteria that influence metabolism . Some researchers believe metformin may also activate AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK), an enzyme involved in cellular energy regulation.

Metformin is taken orally, typically two to three times daily with meals. Extended-release formulations allow for once or twice daily dosing. It has been available in the US since 1995 and is the most widely prescribed diabetes medication in the world, with more than 150 million prescriptions written annually .

Efficacy Comparison: Weight Loss Results

The weight loss gap between these medications is significant.

Semaglutide 2.4 mg (Wegovy) produced average weight loss of 14.9% of body weight in the STEP 1 trial over 68 weeks . For a 220-pound patient, that is roughly 33 pounds. About one-third of participants lost 20% or more.

Metformin's weight loss effects are much more modest. Most studies show average weight loss of 2-5% of body weight, translating to roughly 4-11 pounds for a 220-pound patient . Some patients lose more, some lose nothing, and metformin rarely produces the dramatic results seen with GLP-1 medications.

However, metformin has a different value proposition. It is not primarily a weight loss drug. Its strength lies in blood sugar management, long-term metabolic health, potential cardiovascular protection, and its remarkably low cost and well-established safety profile.

Some physicians prescribe metformin and semaglutide together, as they work through complementary mechanisms and the combination may provide additional metabolic benefits beyond either drug alone .

Another important distinction is how each medication handles weight maintenance. When patients stop semaglutide, most regain a significant portion of lost weight within a year . Metformin, while producing less dramatic weight loss, tends to help maintain a stable weight over time. For patients with insulin resistance, metformin's ongoing improvement of insulin sensitivity can support weight stability even if it does not drive major initial losses.

Side Effects Comparison

Both medications cause GI side effects, but the patterns differ:

Semaglutide side effects:

  • Nausea (20-44%, usually temporary)
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Constipation
  • Abdominal pain
  • Rare: pancreatitis, gallbladder disease, thyroid tumor risk (animal data)

Metformin side effects:

  • Diarrhea (most common, sometimes persistent)
  • Nausea
  • Stomach cramping
  • Metallic taste
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency with long-term use
  • Very rare: lactic acidosis (primarily in patients with kidney impairment)

Metformin's GI side effects are well known and can be persistent, particularly with the immediate-release formulation. The extended-release version (metformin ER) is better tolerated. Unlike semaglutide, metformin does not carry the thyroid tumor or pancreatitis warnings.

Metformin does not cause hypoglycemia when used alone, which is a significant safety advantage. Semaglutide also has a low hypoglycemia risk when used without insulin or sulfonylureas.

Cost Comparison

Metformin is one of the most affordable medications in all of medicine. Generic metformin costs approximately $4 to $30 per month, and many pharmacies include it in their $4 generic programs.

Brand-name semaglutide (Wegovy) costs approximately $1,300-$1,400/mo (brand) per month. Compounded semaglutide through Form Blends runs $150 to $500 per month, which is dramatically cheaper than the brand but still far more expensive than metformin.

For patients on a tight budget, metformin provides metabolic benefits and modest weight loss at a price that is essentially negligible. For patients who need meaningful weight loss and can afford it, semaglutide delivers results that metformin simply cannot match.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I take semaglutide and metformin together?

Yes. Many patients take both medications simultaneously. They work through different mechanisms and complement each other well. Semaglutide provides stronger appetite suppression and weight loss, while metformin contributes to insulin sensitivity and glucose control. This combination is commonly prescribed for patients with type 2 diabetes. In fact, many of the semaglutide clinical trials allowed patients to continue metformin as a background medication, so there is extensive safety data supporting the combination.

Should I switch from metformin to semaglutide?

This depends on your goals. If metformin is managing your blood sugar adequately and you do not need significant weight loss, there may be no reason to switch. If you need more aggressive weight loss or better blood sugar control, adding or switching to semaglutide may be appropriate. Discuss this with your physician.

Is metformin a weight loss drug?

Metformin is not FDA-approved for weight loss. It is approved for type 2 diabetes. However, it can produce modest weight loss as a secondary benefit, and some physicians prescribe it off-label for weight management, particularly in patients with insulin resistance or prediabetes.

Which has fewer side effects?

Both cause GI side effects, but the character differs. Metformin tends to cause diarrhea and stomach cramping, while semaglutide more commonly causes nausea and vomiting. Metformin's side effects can be persistent; semaglutide's usually improve over time. Metformin has a slight edge in long-term safety data simply because it has been studied for decades longer.
