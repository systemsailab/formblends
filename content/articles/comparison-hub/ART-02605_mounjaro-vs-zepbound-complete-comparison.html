Mounjaro Vs Zepbound: Complete Comparison

Mounjaro and Zepbound are essentially the same medication (tirzepatide) with different labels: Mounjaro is approved for type 2 diabetes and Zepbound is approved for weight loss, so your choice depends mainly on your diagnosis and insurance coverage.

If the Ozempic-versus-Wegovy comparison confused you, this one might feel familiar. Just as Novo Nordisk sells semaglutide under two brand names, Eli Lilly sells tirzepatide as both Mounjaro (for diabetes) and Zepbound (for weight management). Same molecule, different packaging, different FDA approvals, and sometimes very different insurance realities. Let us walk through what actually separates them.

How Mounjaro Works

Mounjaro delivers tirzepatide as a once-weekly subcutaneous injection. Tirzepatide is a dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonist, meaning it activates two different incretin hormone receptors simultaneously .

When you eat, your gut releases GLP-1 and GIP to help regulate blood sugar and signal fullness. Tirzepatide amplifies both of these signals far beyond what your body produces naturally. The result is powerful appetite suppression, slower gastric emptying, improved insulin sensitivity, and enhanced fat metabolism.

Mounjaro was designed and studied primarily for blood sugar control in type 2 diabetes patients. The SURPASS clinical trial program demonstrated impressive A1C reductions, with many patients achieving levels below the diabetic threshold. Weight loss in those trials was substantial but was measured as a secondary outcome .

How Zepbound Works

Zepbound uses the exact same tirzepatide molecule at the exact same doses delivered through the exact same type of injection device. The pharmacology is identical because it is the same drug.

What differs is the FDA indication and the clinical data package behind it. Zepbound's approval was based on the SURMOUNT trial program, which studied tirzepatide specifically in people seeking weight loss. These trials enrolled patients based on BMI criteria (30+ or 27+ with comorbidities) rather than diabetes status, and the primary endpoint was percentage of body weight lost.

This matters from a regulatory and insurance perspective, even though the medication itself is the same. Having a weight management indication means that Zepbound can be prescribed on-label for weight loss, which can sometimes make insurance coverage more straightforward.

Efficacy Comparison: Weight Loss Results

Because Mounjaro and Zepbound contain the same active ingredient at the same doses, their weight loss potential is essentially identical when used at equivalent doses in similar patient populations.

The SURMOUNT-1 trial (used for Zepbound's approval) showed weight loss of 15% at 5 mg, 19.5% at 10 mg, and 22.5% at 15 mg over 72 weeks in patients without diabetes . The SURPASS trials (used for Mounjaro's approval) showed similar magnitude of weight loss as a secondary outcome in patients with type 2 diabetes, though the exact percentages varied slightly because of the different patient population.

In practice, whether you take Mounjaro or Zepbound, you are getting tirzepatide. The weight loss you experience will depend on your dose, your adherence, your starting weight, your diet and activity level, and your individual metabolic response. The brand name on the box does not change the biochemistry.

One nuance worth noting: Mounjaro's diabetes trials enrolled patients with type 2 diabetes, who tend to lose slightly less weight on GLP-1 medications than non-diabetic patients. So the weight loss numbers from the SURPASS trials may appear slightly lower than the SURMOUNT numbers, but this reflects the patient population, not a difference in the drug. When non-diabetic patients use Mounjaro off-label for weight loss, they can expect results similar to what SURMOUNT showed for Zepbound.

Not every patient needs to reach the maximum 15 mg dose. Many achieve excellent results at 7.5 mg or 10 mg. Your physician will help find the dose that gives you the best balance of weight loss and tolerability, regardless of which brand name is on the prescription.

Side Effects Comparison

The side effects are identical because the medications are identical:

  • Nausea: The most common side effect, affecting roughly 24-33% of patients in clinical trials .
  • Diarrhea: Reported in approximately 15-23% of patients.
  • Constipation: Affects about 10-17% of patients.
  • Vomiting: Most common during dose escalation periods.
  • Abdominal pain: Related to GI motility changes.
  • Injection site reactions: Generally mild and temporary.

Serious but uncommon risks include pancreatitis, gallbladder disease, kidney injury, and the thyroid C-cell tumor warning from animal studies . Both carry the same boxed warning. Both are contraindicated in patients with medullary thyroid carcinoma or Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Mounjaro and Zepbound the same thing?

They contain the same active ingredient (tirzepatide) at the same doses, made by the same manufacturer. The difference is the FDA-approved indication: Mounjaro for type 2 diabetes, Zepbound for chronic weight management. Pharmacologically, they are identical.

Can I switch between Mounjaro and Zepbound?

Yes, switching is straightforward because they are the same medication. You can transition at the same dose without needing to re-titrate. The main reason patients switch is a change in insurance coverage or formulary status.

Do I need diabetes to get Mounjaro?

Mounjaro is FDA-approved for type 2 diabetes, but physicians can prescribe it off-label for weight management. Insurance coverage for off-label use may be limited, and many patients find it easier to use Zepbound (or compounded tirzepatide) for weight loss purposes.

Is one more effective than the other for weight loss?

No. Because they contain the same molecule at the same doses, their weight loss effectiveness is identical. Any differences in reported outcomes between the SURPASS and SURMOUNT trials reflect differences in the patient populations studied, not differences in the medication.
