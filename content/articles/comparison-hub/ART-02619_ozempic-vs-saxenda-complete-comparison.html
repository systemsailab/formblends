How Ozempic Works

Ozempic delivers {{INTERNAL_LINK:semaglutide}}, a next-generation GLP-1 receptor agonist with a molecular half-life of approximately one week. This long half-life is what enables the once-weekly dosing schedule that most patients prefer over daily injections.

Semaglutide activates GLP-1 receptors in the brain's appetite centers, in the gut to slow gastric emptying, and in the pancreas to improve insulin secretion. The steady-state concentration achieved with weekly dosing provides consistent appetite suppression throughout the week, without the daily peaks and troughs that come with shorter-acting drugs {{CITE:claim}}.

Ozempic is FDA-approved for type 2 diabetes, not weight loss. However, it is one of the most commonly prescribed off-label weight loss medications in the country. Its weight-loss-specific sibling, {{INTERNAL_LINK:Wegovy}}, uses the same molecule at a slightly higher maximum dose (2.4 mg vs 2 mg).

How Saxenda Works

Saxenda delivers liraglutide, a first-generation GLP-1 receptor agonist with a half-life of approximately 13 hours {{CITE:claim}}. This shorter duration means it must be injected every day to maintain therapeutic levels.

Liraglutide activates the same GLP-1 receptors as semaglutide and produces the same general effects: appetite suppression, delayed gastric emptying, and improved blood sugar regulation. However, the daily dosing cycle creates a pattern where drug levels peak a few hours after injection and then decline throughout the day.

Saxenda was approved by the FDA in December 2014 specifically for chronic weight management. It was groundbreaking at the time, proving that GLP-1 medications could serve as effective weight loss tools. Saxenda is approved for adults with BMI 30+ or BMI 27+ with at least one weight-related condition, and it also has approval for adolescents aged 12 and older {{CITE:claim}}.

Efficacy Comparison: Weight Loss Results

Ozempic, even at its lower diabetes-oriented doses, generally outperforms Saxenda for weight loss.

In the SCALE trial, Saxenda 3 mg daily produced average weight loss of approximately 8% of body weight over 56 weeks {{CITE:claim}}. About 33% of patients achieved at least 10% weight loss.

Ozempic data from the SUSTAIN trials shows weight loss of approximately 10-14% at the 1 mg dose, and even more at the 2 mg dose, over similar timeframes {{CITE:claim}}. These are diabetes trial results, where weight loss was a secondary endpoint. When semaglutide was studied specifically for weight loss at the 2.4 mg dose (as Wegovy), results climbed to nearly 15%.

The head-to-head STEP 8 trial compared semaglutide 2.4 mg to liraglutide 3.0 mg directly and found semaglutide produced 15.8% weight loss versus 6.4% for liraglutide {{CITE:claim}}. While this comparison used Wegovy-level dosing rather than Ozempic doses, it confirms the fundamental superiority of semaglutide over liraglutide as a molecule.

The bottom line: even Ozempic at its diabetes doses produces more weight loss than Saxenda at its full weight loss dose.

Beyond the raw numbers, the quality and sustainability of weight loss matter. Semaglutide's longer half-life provides more consistent drug levels throughout the week, which means more consistent appetite suppression. Liraglutide's daily cycle means drug levels peak and then drop each day, which some patients notice as varying hunger levels throughout the day. The steady-state pharmacokinetics of semaglutide appear to produce a more uniform appetite-suppressing experience.

Side Effects Comparison

Both medications produce similar types of side effects, which is expected since they work on the same receptor:

  • Nausea: Common with both. May be slightly more intense with semaglutide but occurs less frequently (weekly dosing means fewer peak-related episodes).
  • Vomiting: Reported with both, especially during dose increases.
  • Diarrhea and constipation: Comparable rates.
  • Abdominal pain: Related to gastric emptying changes; similar for both.
  • Headache and fatigue: Reported as minor side effects with both.
  • Injection burden: Saxenda requires 365 injections per year; Ozempic requires 52. For many patients, this practical difference significantly affects quality of life and adherence.

Both carry the same serious risk warnings: pancreatitis, gallbladder disease, kidney problems, and thyroid C-cell tumor risk from animal studies {{CITE:claim}}. Both are contraindicated in patients with medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN-2 syndrome.

Adherence is notably better with once-weekly medications. Studies consistently show that patients are more likely to stay on weekly GLP-1 medications than daily ones {{CITE:claim}}, which translates to better real-world outcomes.
