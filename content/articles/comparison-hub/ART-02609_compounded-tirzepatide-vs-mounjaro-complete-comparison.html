How Mounjaro Works

Mounjaro is Eli Lilly's brand-name {{INTERNAL_LINK:tirzepatide}} product, approved by the FDA in May 2022 for the treatment of type 2 diabetes. It is a dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonist, meaning it activates two different incretin hormone receptors to produce powerful metabolic effects {{CITE:claim}}.

The GLP-1 component suppresses appetite, slows gastric emptying, and enhances insulin secretion. The GIP component adds additional benefits for fat metabolism and insulin sensitivity. Together, these two mechanisms produce greater weight loss and blood sugar improvement than either pathway alone.

Mounjaro is delivered through a prefilled, single-dose injection pen. Patients inject once weekly, starting at 2.5 mg and increasing every four weeks through six dose levels up to 15 mg. The pen device is designed for ease of use, with a hidden needle and a simple push-button mechanism.

How Compounded Tirzepatide Works

{{INTERNAL_LINK:Compounded tirzepatide}} contains the same tirzepatide molecule used in Mounjaro. The active pharmaceutical ingredient is sourced from FDA-registered suppliers and then formulated by a licensed compounding pharmacy into an injectable preparation.

The mechanism of action is identical. Compounded tirzepatide activates both GIP and GLP-1 receptors in the same way as Mounjaro, producing the same metabolic effects: appetite reduction, slower digestion, improved insulin response, and enhanced fat metabolism.

The main practical differences are in the delivery system and dosing flexibility. Compounded tirzepatide is typically supplied in a multi-dose vial rather than a prefilled pen. Patients draw up their prescribed dose using an insulin syringe. While this requires a few extra steps, it also allows for more granular dose adjustments. A physician can prescribe any dose, not just the six fixed levels that Mounjaro offers.

Side Effects Comparison

Side effects are determined by the molecule, not the manufacturer. Both compounded tirzepatide and Mounjaro carry the same risk profile:

  • Nausea: Affects 24-33% of patients, most common during titration {{CITE:claim}}.
  • Diarrhea: Reported in 15-23% of patients.
  • Constipation: Affects 10-17% of patients.
  • Vomiting: Usually occurs during dose increases.
  • Decreased appetite: Very common and is the intended therapeutic effect.
  • Injection site reactions: Possible with both delivery methods.

Serious but rare risks include pancreatitis, gallbladder problems, kidney injury, and the thyroid C-cell tumor warning based on animal studies. These are risks of tirzepatide as a molecule and apply equally to both products {{CITE:claim}}.
