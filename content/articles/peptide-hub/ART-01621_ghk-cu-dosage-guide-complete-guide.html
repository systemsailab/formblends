GHK-Cu Dosage Guide: Complete Guide

Quick Answer: GHK-Cu dosages used in research and clinical settings typically range from 1 mg to 4 mg per day via subcutaneous injection. There is no universally established human dosing protocol, and optimal dosing should be determined by a licensed physician based on your individual health profile and goals {{CITE:GHK-Cu dosages in research typically range from 1 mg to 4 mg per day via subcutaneous injection}}.

What Is GHK-Cu?

GHK-Cu (glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine copper complex) is a naturally occurring tripeptide bound to a copper ion. It was first identified in human plasma in 1973 and has since been studied extensively for its roles in wound healing, collagen production, anti-aging gene modulation, and tissue repair {{CITE:GHK-Cu was first identified in human plasma in 1973}}.

GHK-Cu is available in both topical and injectable forms. This guide focuses primarily on subcutaneous injectable dosing, as this is the route most commonly used in physician-supervised peptide therapy protocols. Topical applications have their own dosing considerations and are briefly addressed below.

GHK-Cu Dosing Ranges

Because GHK-Cu is not FDA-approved for any condition, there is no official prescribing information or standardized dosing schedule. The following ranges are drawn from research literature and clinical protocols used by physicians experienced with peptide therapy.

Subcutaneous Injection

The most common dosing range for subcutaneous GHK-Cu injections is:

  • Starting dose: 1 mg per day
  • Standard dose: 2 mg per day
  • Higher dose: 3 to 4 mg per day (used in some protocols for specific goals)

Most protocols call for once-daily injection, typically administered in the morning or evening. Some physicians prefer twice-daily dosing at lower amounts (for example, 1 mg in the morning and 1 mg in the evening) to maintain more stable peptide levels throughout the day.

Topical Application

Topical GHK-Cu products vary widely in concentration. Formulations used in clinical research have typically contained GHK-Cu at concentrations between 0.01% and 1% {{CITE:Clinical research has used topical GHK-Cu at concentrations between 0.01% and 1%}}. Topical use is generally considered to have a more localized effect compared to injectable administration.

Factors That Influence Dosing

Your physician will consider several factors when determining your GHK-Cu dose:

  • Body weight: Larger individuals may require doses at the higher end of the range.
  • Health goals: Skin rejuvenation, wound healing, and anti-aging protocols may call for different dosing strategies.
  • Age: Natural GHK-Cu levels decline significantly with age, which may inform starting doses.
  • Tolerance: Individual response to GHK-Cu varies. Starting at a lower dose allows assessment of tolerability before increasing.
  • Other peptides: If GHK-Cu is being used alongside other peptides, your physician may adjust doses accordingly.

Reconstitution

GHK-Cu is typically supplied as a lyophilized (freeze-dried) powder that must be reconstituted with bacteriostatic water before injection. Here is the general process:

Step-by-Step Reconstitution

  1. Gather materials: GHK-Cu vial, bacteriostatic water, alcohol swabs, and a sterile syringe with needle.
  2. Clean the vial tops: Wipe the rubber stoppers of both the GHK-Cu vial and the bacteriostatic water vial with alcohol swabs.
  3. Draw the diluent: Using a sterile syringe, draw the appropriate amount of bacteriostatic water. A common reconstitution volume is 2 mL of bacteriostatic water per 5 mg vial, which yields a concentration of 2.5 mg per mL.
  4. Add water slowly: Insert the needle into the GHK-Cu vial and direct the stream of water against the glass wall, not directly onto the powder. Let the water run down the side of the vial to avoid damaging the peptide.
  5. Mix gently: Swirl the vial gently until the powder is fully dissolved. Do not shake vigorously, as this can degrade the peptide.
  6. Verify clarity: The resulting solution should be clear. If it appears cloudy or contains particles, do not use it.

Reconstitution Math

Understanding the concentration after reconstitution is essential for accurate dosing:

  • 5 mg vial + 2 mL bacteriostatic water = 2.5 mg/mL (each 0.1 mL contains 0.25 mg)
  • 5 mg vial + 1 mL bacteriostatic water = 5 mg/mL (each 0.1 mL contains 0.5 mg)
  • 10 mg vial + 2 mL bacteriostatic water = 5 mg/mL (each 0.1 mL contains 0.5 mg)

Your physician or pharmacy will provide specific reconstitution instructions based on the vial concentration you receive.

Cycling Protocols

Many physicians recommend cycling GHK-Cu rather than using it continuously. Common cycling patterns include:

  • Standard cycle: 4 to 6 weeks on, followed by 2 to 4 weeks off
  • Extended cycle: 8 to 12 weeks on, followed by 4 weeks off
  • Maintenance cycle: After an initial treatment period, some protocols call for reduced frequency (such as 3 days per week) for ongoing maintenance

Cycling serves two purposes. First, it may help prevent receptor desensitization, where the body becomes less responsive to the peptide over time. Second, it allows periodic assessment of how the body responds without the peptide, which helps your physician evaluate the ongoing need for therapy.

The ideal cycling protocol depends on your health goals and your physician's clinical judgment. There is no single "correct" cycle for all individuals.

Storage

Proper storage is essential to maintaining GHK-Cu potency:

  • Before reconstitution: Store lyophilized GHK-Cu in the refrigerator (36 to 46 degrees Fahrenheit / 2 to 8 degrees Celsius). Unreconstituted peptide is generally stable for extended periods under proper refrigeration.
  • After reconstitution: Refrigerate immediately. Reconstituted GHK-Cu should be used within 28 days. Never freeze reconstituted peptide solution.
  • Avoid heat and light: Keep vials away from direct sunlight and temperatures above room temperature.
  • Travel: Use an insulated bag with cold packs for transport. Avoid leaving the vial in a hot car or uncontrolled environment.

Safety and Side Effects

GHK-Cu is a naturally occurring peptide in the human body, which contributes to its favorable safety profile. Side effects are generally mild and may include injection site reactions, temporary flushing, mild nausea, and occasional headache {{CITE:GHK-Cu side effects are generally mild and include injection site reactions, flushing, and nausea}}.

Dosing outside the recommended range or without physician guidance increases the risk of adverse effects. Do not self-adjust your dose without consulting your provider.
