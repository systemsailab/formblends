What Is Melanotan II?

Melanotan II (MT-II) is a synthetic peptide developed at the University of Arizona in the 1990s. It mimics alpha-melanocyte-stimulating hormone (alpha-MSH), a natural hormone that tells your melanocytes (pigment-producing skin cells) to make melanin {{CITE:Melanotan II developed at University of Arizona as alpha-MSH analog}}.

Unlike the original Melanotan I (now marketed as Scenesse for a rare skin condition), Melanotan II is non-selective. It activates all five melanocortin receptor subtypes, which is why it produces effects beyond tanning, including appetite changes, sexual arousal, and drowsiness.

What to Expect: Week by Week

Week 1

Side effects are most noticeable during the first week. Expect nausea (50-80% of users), facial flushing, and possible drowsiness. Skin changes are not yet visible. You are getting comfortable with the injection process.

Week 2

Nausea typically begins to subside. You may notice freckles darkening and a subtle warmth to your skin tone, especially on the face and arms. Appetite suppression becomes noticeable.

Week 3-4

Visible tanning is apparent. Side effects have significantly diminished for most users. You approach your desired pigmentation level and prepare to switch to maintenance dosing.

Essential Beginner Tips

  • Always inject before bed. You will sleep through peak nausea and flushing.
  • Start low. Begin at 100 mcg regardless of your eventual target dose. Assess tolerance first.
  • Get a baseline skin check. See a dermatologist for a full-body mole exam before starting. This is non-negotiable.
  • Buy a sharps container. Proper needle disposal is required, not optional.
  • Keep a log. Track dose, side effects, and skin changes daily for the first month.
  • Use sunscreen. Melanotan II provides some UV protection, but not enough to replace sunscreen.
  • Only use pharmaceutical-grade product. Peptides labeled "research use only" are not manufactured for human use.

Common Beginner Mistakes

  • Starting too high: Jumping to 500 mcg on day one causes severe nausea. Always titrate up.
  • Skipping dermatological monitoring: Mole changes are a real concern. Do not skip baseline and follow-up skin checks.
  • Expecting instant results: Melanogenesis takes days to weeks. Patience is required.
  • Using nasal sprays instead of injection: Unvalidated bioavailability makes dosing unreliable.
  • Not refrigerating after reconstitution: Room temperature degrades the peptide rapidly.
