Hormone Optimization Men Over 40: How To Start

Starting hormone optimization as a man over 40 begins with three actions: get a comprehensive blood panel to know your baseline, start lifting heavy things three times per week, and fix your sleep to give your body the recovery window it needs to produce hormones. These three steps address the root causes of hormonal decline and create the conditions for natural restoration . You do not need exotic protocols. You need consistent basics executed well.

Step 1: Get Your Blood Work Done

You cannot optimize what you cannot measure. Many men over 40 have never had a comprehensive hormone panel. Standard annual physicals rarely test beyond cholesterol and blood sugar.

The Tests You Need

  • Total testosterone: Your overall production level. Request an early morning draw (before 9 AM) when levels peak.
  • Free testosterone: The bioavailable form that actually exerts effects on your tissues. More clinically relevant than total testosterone alone.
  • SHBG (sex hormone binding globulin): Binds testosterone and makes it inactive. High SHBG means less free testosterone available.
  • Estradiol (E2): Too high indicates excessive aromatase conversion. Too low causes joint pain and mood issues.
  • DHEA-S: A precursor hormone that supports testosterone and acts as a buffer against cortisol.
  • Thyroid panel: TSH, free T3, free T4. Thyroid dysfunction mimics low testosterone symptoms.
  • Fasting insulin and glucose: Insulin resistance is a major driver of hormonal decline.
  • Vitamin D: Low vitamin D directly correlates with low testosterone.
  • hs-CRP: Systemic inflammation marker. Chronic inflammation suppresses testosterone production.

How to Get Tested

  • Ask your primary care doctor for a comprehensive panel. Many will order it, especially if you report symptoms.

  • Fast for 12 hours before the draw. Schedule for early morning (7 to 9 AM).
  • Avoid alcohol for 48 hours and intense exercise for 24 hours before testing, as both can temporarily skew results.

Once you have results, you have a roadmap. Low testosterone? Focus on sleep, body fat, and resistance training. High cortisol? Prioritize stress management. Insulin resistance? Dietary changes and exercise become urgent priorities .

Step 2: Start Lifting Weights

Resistance training is the single most effective natural testosterone booster for men over 40. Compound movements that recruit large muscle groups trigger the strongest hormonal response. Studies consistently show that men who lift weights have significantly higher testosterone levels than sedentary men of the same age .

Beginner Program (Weeks 1 Through 4)

If you are new to lifting or returning after a long break, start here:

Day Exercises Sets x Reps
Monday Goblet squat, dumbbell bench press, dumbbell row, plank 3 x 10 to 12 each
Wednesday Romanian deadlift, overhead press, lat pulldown, farmer's walk 3 x 10 to 12 each
Friday Leg press, incline dumbbell press, cable row, carry variation 3 x 10 to 12 each
  • Start with weights you can control for all reps with good form.
  • Add weight gradually (2.5 to 5 lbs per exercise per week when possible).
  • Focus on the eccentric (lowering) phase: 2 to 3 seconds down, 1 second up.
  • Rest 90 seconds to 2 minutes between sets of compound movements.

Why Compound Lifts Matter for Hormones

Exercises that use multiple joints and large muscle groups (squats, deadlifts, presses, rows) produce a greater hormonal response than isolation exercises (bicep curls, leg extensions). The more muscle mass involved, the stronger the signal to your endocrine system .

On non-lifting days, walk. Aim for 7,000 to 10,000 steps. Walking supports insulin sensitivity, lowers cortisol, and provides active recovery without adding stress to the system.

Step 3: Fix Your Sleep

Testosterone production happens primarily during deep sleep. If you are sleeping 5 to 6 hours per night, you are actively sabotaging your hormones. One week of 5-hour sleep nights reduces testosterone by 10 to 15% in young men; the effect is even more pronounced in older men who already have declining production .

Quick Sleep Fixes

  • Commit to 7 to 8 hours in bed, lights out to alarm.
  • Consistent bedtime and wake time, seven days a week. Social jet lag is a testosterone killer.
  • Get morning sunlight within 30 minutes of waking. This sets your circadian clock for proper melatonin release at night.
  • No caffeine after noon.
  • Make the bedroom dark, cool (66 to 68 degrees F), and quiet.
  • Take 300 to 400 mg magnesium glycinate 30 to 60 minutes before bed sleep optimization biohacking.

For many men, fixing sleep alone produces noticeable improvements in energy, mood, and body composition within 2 to 3 weeks.

Step 4: Clean Up Your Diet (Week 2 Onward)

You do not need a perfect diet. You need to stop doing the things that actively harm your hormones and start doing the things that support them.

Stop Doing

  • Drinking more than 2 alcoholic beverages on any day. Alcohol increases aromatase activity and directly suppresses testicular testosterone production .
  • Eating very low fat (below 20% of calories). Cholesterol is the raw material for testosterone synthesis.
  • Relying on processed food for most meals. Processed foods drive inflammation, insulin resistance, and nutrient deficiencies.
  • Skipping protein at meals. You need 1.2 to 1.6 g of protein per kg of bodyweight daily, spread across meals.

Start Doing

  • Eat 2 to 3 servings of fatty fish per week (salmon, sardines, mackerel) for omega-3s and vitamin D.
  • Include zinc-rich foods: oysters (the single best food source of zinc), beef, pumpkin seeds, and lentils.
  • Add cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts) 3 to 5 times per week. They contain DIM and I3C, which support healthy estrogen metabolism .
  • Cook with olive oil and eat avocados, nuts, and eggs for healthy fats.
  • If body fat is above 20%, create a moderate caloric deficit (300 to 500 calories below maintenance). Fat loss is one of the most powerful natural testosterone boosters GLP-1 weight loss.

When to Seek Medical Help

Lifestyle optimization has limits. Consider medical consultation if:

  • Total testosterone remains below 350 ng/dL after 12 weeks of consistent lifestyle optimization.
  • Symptoms persist or worsen despite measurable improvements in body composition and lifestyle.
  • Lab results suggest primary hypogonadism (high LH/FSH with low testosterone), indicating a testicular production issue rather than a lifestyle one.
  • Thyroid antibodies are elevated, suggesting autoimmune thyroiditis requiring medical management.

I am 42 and feel fine. Should I still get tested?

Yes. Many hormonal declines are gradual enough that you adapt to feeling suboptimal without realizing it. Baseline testing at 40 provides a reference point for the future. You may find that "fine" becomes "great" with targeted optimization.

How important is reducing alcohol?

Very. Alcohol is one of the most potent testosterone suppressors in the average man's lifestyle. Even moderate daily drinking (2 drinks) can measurably lower testosterone. Reducing to 3 to 4 drinks per week maximum is one of the simplest and most impactful changes you can make .

Will losing belly fat really help my testosterone?

Yes. Visceral fat contains high levels of aromatase, the enzyme that converts testosterone to estrogen. Losing 10 to 15 pounds of fat can produce a measurable increase in testosterone levels, sometimes equivalent to the effect of pharmaceutical intervention .

Is this safe for men with high blood pressure or heart conditions?

The lifestyle elements of this protocol (exercise, nutrition, sleep, stress reduction) generally improve cardiovascular health. However, men with existing conditions should work with their physician on exercise intensity and supplement choices. Certain supplements may interact with blood pressure or cardiac medications .

How is this different from testosterone replacement therapy (TRT)?

This protocol aims to optimize your body's own testosterone production through lifestyle and supplementation. TRT provides external testosterone, which can suppress natural production.
