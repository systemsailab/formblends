How GLP-1 Medications Work

GLP-1 receptor agonists mimic a hormone called glucagon-like peptide-1 that your body already produces. This hormone plays a key role in regulating appetite and blood sugar. When you take a GLP-1 medication, the effect is amplified: you feel fuller faster, cravings decrease, and the constant mental pull toward food loosens its grip.

These medications are not appetite suppressants in the traditional sense. They do not wire you up or make you jittery. They work with your body's existing systems to restore a more balanced relationship between hunger and satiety. For people who have spent years feeling like their appetite is working against them, the shift can be profound.

Clinical data backs this up. Studies on semaglutide and tirzepatide have shown average weight loss of 15% to over 20% of body weight. That puts these medications in a category of effectiveness that was previously only achievable through bariatric surgery.
