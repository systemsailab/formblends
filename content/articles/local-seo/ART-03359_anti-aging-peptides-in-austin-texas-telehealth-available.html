What Are Anti-Aging Peptides?

Peptides are short chains of amino acids that act as signaling molecules in your body. They tell cells what to do. As you age, your natural peptide production declines, contributing to visible signs of aging like wrinkles, thinning skin, and slower recovery, as well as internal changes like reduced energy and poor sleep quality. {{CITE:Role of peptides in cellular signaling and aging, Journal of Clinical Endocrinology}}

Anti-aging peptide therapy introduces specific peptides into your body to restore or enhance functions that diminish with age. These are not synthetic hormones. They work with your body's existing systems to stimulate natural processes like collagen production, cellular repair, and growth hormone release.

Common Anti-Aging Peptides

  • GHK-Cu: A copper peptide known for promoting collagen synthesis, skin repair, and wound healing. Research suggests it can tighten skin, reduce fine lines, and improve skin elasticity. {{CITE:GHK-Cu peptide skin regeneration studies, Journal of Biological Chemistry}}
  • Epithalon: A synthetic version of the naturally occurring peptide epithalamin, studied for its potential to activate telomerase, the enzyme responsible for maintaining telomere length. {{CITE:Epithalon telomerase activation research, Bulletin of Experimental Biology and Medicine}}
  • CJC-1295/Ipamorelin: A combination that stimulates your pituitary gland to produce more growth hormone, supporting muscle maintenance, fat metabolism, and recovery. {{INTERNAL_LINK:growth hormone peptides}}
  • Thymosin Beta-4: Plays a role in tissue repair, reducing inflammation, and promoting cell migration for healing.

How are the peptides administered?

Most anti-aging peptides are administered via subcutaneous injection, which is a small, shallow injection just under the skin. Some peptides are available in topical or oral forms. Your provider will explain the administration method for your specific protocol.
