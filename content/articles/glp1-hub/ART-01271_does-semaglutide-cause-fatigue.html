Does Semaglutide Cause Fatigue?

Semaglutide can cause fatigue in some patients. Clinical trials report fatigue or tiredness in approximately 5 to 11 percent of participants, making it a recognized but less common side effect compared to gastrointestinal symptoms. It is most likely during the first weeks of treatment and during dose escalation.

Detailed Explanation

Semaglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist prescribed under brand names like Ozempic (for type 2 diabetes) and Wegovy (for weight management). While nausea, diarrhea, and constipation are the most frequently discussed side effects, fatigue is consistently listed in prescribing information and was reported by a notable subset of clinical trial participants.

How Common Is Fatigue on Semaglutide?

In the STEP clinical trial program for Wegovy, fatigue was reported by approximately 11 percent of participants taking the 2.4 mg dose compared to about 5 percent in the placebo group. In the SUSTAIN trials for Ozempic at lower doses, the rate was somewhat lower but still present. The difference between treatment and placebo groups suggests the medication itself contributes to fatigue in some individuals rather than it being purely coincidental.

Why Semaglutide May Cause Fatigue

Several mechanisms may explain semaglutide-related fatigue. The most straightforward is reduced caloric intake. Semaglutide significantly decreases appetite, and when patients eat substantially fewer calories than their body is accustomed to, low energy and tiredness are common consequences. The body must adapt to using stored fat for fuel, and this metabolic transition takes time.

Gastrointestinal side effects like nausea and vomiting can also contribute indirectly. Patients who experience persistent nausea often eat less and may become mildly dehydrated, both of which worsen fatigue. Blood sugar fluctuations, particularly in patients with diabetes, can also play a role, as semaglutide lowers blood glucose levels and occasional dips may produce tiredness.

When Does Fatigue Typically Occur?

Fatigue is most common during the first four to eight weeks of treatment and during each dose increase. As the body adjusts to the new caloric intake and the medication's effects on digestion and metabolism, energy levels typically stabilize. Most patients find that fatigue diminishes significantly after the first two to three months of treatment.

Fatigue vs. More Serious Symptoms

Mild fatigue is generally not a cause for concern. However, severe or persistent exhaustion, fatigue accompanied by rapid heart rate, or extreme weakness may indicate dehydration, nutritional deficiency, or another underlying issue that warrants medical evaluation. Patients should distinguish between manageable tiredness and symptoms that interfere with daily functioning.

What to Consider

  • Ensure adequate caloric and protein intake even while appetite is reduced. Eating too little can directly cause fatigue.
  • Stay well hydrated throughout the day, especially if you experience nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea.
  • Prioritize nutrient-dense foods including lean protein, complex carbohydrates, and healthy fats to maintain stable energy levels.
  • Report persistent or severe fatigue to your prescribing physician, as dose adjustment or additional evaluation may be needed.
  • Fatigue often improves as your body adapts to the medication over the first several weeks.
  • Consider having blood work done if fatigue persists, as vitamin or mineral deficiencies can develop with reduced food intake.
