Semaglutide Insurance Coverage: Complete Guide 2026

Insurance coverage for semaglutide varies widely depending on your plan type, the specific brand prescribed, and the medical indication. As of 2026, most commercial insurance plans cover Ozempic for type 2 diabetes, but coverage for Wegovy (the weight management formulation) remains inconsistent. Understanding your coverage options can save you hundreds or even thousands of dollars per month. In this guide, we break down the current insurance landscape for semaglutide so you know exactly what to expect.

Understanding the Two Brands of Semaglutide

Before diving into coverage details, it helps to understand that semaglutide is sold under two main brand names, each with different insurance implications.

Ozempic (Semaglutide for Type 2 Diabetes)

Ozempic is FDA-approved for improving blood sugar control in adults with type 2 diabetes. It comes in doses of 0.5 mg, 1.0 mg, and 2.0 mg. Because type 2 diabetes is widely recognized as a medical condition requiring treatment, insurance coverage for Ozempic is significantly more common.

Wegovy (Semaglutide for Weight Management)

Wegovy is FDA-approved for chronic weight management in adults with a BMI of 30 or higher, or 27 or higher with at least one weight-related comorbidity. The maintenance dose is 2.4 mg weekly. Despite strong clinical evidence supporting its use, many insurance plans still classify weight management drugs as non-essential or elective, which limits coverage.

Coverage by Insurance Plan Type

Your type of insurance plan is the biggest factor in determining whether semaglutide will be covered and at what cost to you.

Employer-Sponsored Commercial Insurance

Approximately 40 to 50 percent of large employer-sponsored plans now offer some coverage for anti-obesity medications, up from roughly 25 percent in 2022. However, coverage often comes with requirements:

  • Prior authorization is almost always required
  • Step therapy may be mandated, meaning you must try and fail cheaper medications first
  • Quantity limits may restrict the duration of coverage
  • Some plans cover the medication but place it on a high-cost specialty tier with significant copays

For Ozempic specifically, coverage rates are higher. Most commercial plans include it on their formulary for type 2 diabetes, though tier placement and copay amounts vary.

Medicare

Medicare Part D has historically excluded coverage for weight loss medications. However, the landscape shifted with the passage of the Treat and Reduce Obesity Act provisions and CMS updates.

As of 2026, Medicare coverage for semaglutide depends on the indication:

  • For type 2 diabetes (Ozempic): Generally covered under Part D, subject to formulary placement and copays
  • For weight management (Wegovy): Limited coverage becoming available following legislative changes, but not universally covered across all Part D plans yet

Medicaid

Medicaid coverage for semaglutide varies by state. Some state Medicaid programs cover Ozempic for diabetes but exclude Wegovy for weight management. Others have begun adding anti-obesity medications following CMS guidance encouraging coverage.

Marketplace (ACA) Plans

ACA marketplace plans are not required to cover anti-obesity medications as part of Essential Health Benefits. Coverage varies significantly by plan and by state. If your marketplace plan does cover semaglutide, it will likely require prior authorization and may have high cost-sharing.

Military and VA Coverage

TRICARE covers Ozempic for type 2 diabetes and has added Wegovy to its formulary for qualifying patients. The VA formulary includes semaglutide for diabetes, with weight management use evaluated on a case-by-case basis.

How to Check Your Coverage

Before starting semaglutide, take these steps to understand your coverage.

Call Your Insurance Company

Call the member services number on your insurance card and ask these specific questions:

  1. Is semaglutide (Ozempic or Wegovy) on your formulary?
  2. What tier is it placed on, and what is my copay or coinsurance?
  3. Is prior authorization required?
  4. Are there step therapy requirements?
  5. Are there quantity limits or duration limits on coverage?
  6. Does coverage depend on the diagnosis (diabetes vs. obesity)?

Check the Formulary Online

Most insurance plans publish their formulary online. Search for "semaglutide," "Ozempic," or "Wegovy" to see if it is listed and on which tier. Higher tiers mean higher out-of-pocket costs.

Prior Authorization: What You Need to Know

Prior authorization (PA) is the most common hurdle for semaglutide coverage. Your insurance company requires your doctor to submit documentation proving that the medication is medically necessary before they will approve coverage.

Common PA Requirements

  • Documented BMI of 30 or higher, or 27 or higher with comorbidities
  • Evidence of previous weight loss attempts (dietary counseling, exercise programs, other medications)
  • Documentation of weight-related comorbidities (hypertension, dyslipidemia, sleep apnea, type 2 diabetes)
  • Recent lab work (metabolic panel, HbA1c for diabetes indication)

How Long Does Prior Authorization Take?

Standard PA decisions typically take 5 to 15 business days. Urgent or expedited reviews can be completed in 24 to 72 hours if your physician requests it. Some plans offer electronic PA, which can be faster.

What If Prior Authorization Is Denied?

A denial is not the end of the road. You have the right to appeal, and many initial denials are overturned on appeal. More on the appeal process below.

How to Appeal a Coverage Denial

If your insurance denies coverage for semaglutide, you can and should appeal. Here is a step-by-step approach.

Step 1: Understand the Reason for Denial

Request a written denial letter that explains the specific reason your claim was denied. Common reasons include:

  • Medication not on formulary
  • Prior authorization criteria not met
  • Step therapy requirement not fulfilled
  • Obesity classified as a non-covered condition

Step 2: Gather Supporting Documentation

Work with your physician to compile a strong appeal package. Include:

  • A letter of medical necessity from your prescribing physician
  • Relevant clinical trial data showing semaglutide's effectiveness
  • Documentation of failed previous treatments
  • Evidence of weight-related health conditions and their severity
  • Guidelines from medical organizations supporting pharmacotherapy for obesity (AMA, Obesity Medicine Association, Endocrine Society)

Step 3: Submit the Internal Appeal

Most insurance plans allow two levels of internal appeal. Submit your appeal within the timeframe specified in your denial letter, usually 30 to 180 days.

Step 4: Request an External Review

If internal appeals are denied, you have the right to an independent external review by a third-party organization. External reviewers overturn denials in a meaningful percentage of cases.

Step 5: Contact Your State Insurance Commissioner

If you believe your denial violates state insurance regulations, file a complaint with your state's department of insurance. Several states have passed or are considering laws requiring coverage for anti-obesity medications.

State Laws Affecting Semaglutide Coverage

The legislative landscape around anti-obesity medication coverage is changing rapidly. Several states have enacted or proposed laws that impact semaglutide coverage.

States With Anti-Obesity Medication Mandates

A growing number of states now require certain insurance plans to cover FDA-approved anti-obesity medications. These mandates vary in scope but generally apply to fully insured plans (not self-funded employer plans).

Self-Funded Plans and ERISA

If your employer self-funds their health plan, state insurance mandates may not apply due to federal ERISA preemption. Approximately 65% of workers with employer coverage are in self-funded plans. For these plans, coverage decisions are made by the employer, not regulated by state law.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does insurance cover semaglutide for weight loss?

Some plans do, but coverage is far from universal. Roughly 40 to 50 percent of large employer plans include anti-obesity medications. You will need to check your specific plan's formulary and requirements.

Is Ozempic easier to get covered than Wegovy?

Yes, significantly. Because Ozempic is approved for type 2 diabetes, a condition that insurance plans universally cover, it has much broader coverage. Wegovy's weight management indication faces more barriers.

Can my doctor prescribe Ozempic off-label for weight loss to get insurance coverage?

Physicians can prescribe medications off-label, but insurance companies are not required to cover off-label use. If your doctor prescribes Ozempic for weight loss, your insurer may deny the claim if you do not have a type 2 diabetes diagnosis.

How much does a prior authorization cost me?

There is no direct cost to you for a prior authorization. Your physician's office handles the paperwork. However, the process can delay getting your medication by 1 to 3 weeks.

What if I change insurance plans?

If you switch insurance plans, you may need to go through the prior authorization process again with your new insurer. Coverage levels, copays, and formulary placement may differ.

Are there any Medicare Advantage plans that cover Wegovy?

Some Medicare Advantage plans offer supplemental benefits that include anti-obesity medications. Coverage varies by plan and region. Review plan details during open enrollment to compare options.
