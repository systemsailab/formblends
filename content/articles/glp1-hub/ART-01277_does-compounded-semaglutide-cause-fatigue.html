Does Compounded Semaglutide Cause Fatigue?

Compounded semaglutide can cause fatigue, just as brand-name semaglutide (Ozempic and Wegovy) can. Because the active ingredient is the same, the side effect profile, including fatigue in approximately 5 to 11 percent of users, is expected to be comparable. Tiredness is most common during early treatment and dose changes.

Detailed Explanation

Compounded semaglutide is prepared by compounding pharmacies and offers the same active molecule as brand-name Ozempic and Wegovy. It became widely available as an alternative during supply shortages and as a more affordable option for patients paying out of pocket. While compounded versions are not FDA-approved products, they contain semaglutide and produce the same pharmacological effects, including appetite suppression, blood sugar reduction, and the associated side effects.

Is Fatigue the Same as With Brand-Name Semaglutide?

The fatigue experienced on compounded semaglutide is driven by the same mechanisms as brand-name semaglutide: reduced caloric intake due to appetite suppression, metabolic adaptation during weight loss, and potential dehydration from GI side effects. There is no clinical reason to expect that fatigue would be more or less common with a properly compounded product at equivalent doses.

However, because compounded medications can vary in formulation (some use semaglutide sodium salt, and some include additional ingredients like B12 or L-carnitine), individual experiences may differ slightly. A compounded formulation that includes vitamin B12 may theoretically help offset some fatigue, though this has not been studied in clinical trials.

Factors Specific to Compounded Semaglutide

One consideration unique to compounded semaglutide is dosing precision. Compounding pharmacies may use different concentration preparations, and patients switching between compounded and brand-name versions (or between different compounding pharmacies) may notice variations in side effects including fatigue. Working with a prescriber who is experienced with compounded semaglutide helps ensure consistent dosing and minimizes unexpected side effects.

Some compounding pharmacies offer semaglutide in sublingual or oral forms rather than injection. The bioavailability and absorption timing may differ with these formulations, which could affect how side effects manifest. Patients using non-injectable compounded semaglutide should discuss expected side effects, including fatigue, with their prescribing physician.

Managing Fatigue on Compounded Semaglutide

The management strategies for fatigue on compounded semaglutide are identical to those for brand-name versions. Adequate protein intake, sufficient hydration, gradual dose escalation, and consistent meal timing are the most effective approaches. Patients should also ensure they are sourcing their compounded semaglutide from a reputable 503A or 503B pharmacy that follows current Good Manufacturing Practices.
