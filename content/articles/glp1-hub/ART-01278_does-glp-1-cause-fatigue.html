Does GLP-1 Cause Fatigue?

GLP-1 receptor agonist medications can cause fatigue in some patients. Across the drug class, fatigue is reported by approximately 5 to 11 percent of users, depending on the specific medication and dose. It is one of the more common non-gastrointestinal side effects and typically occurs during early treatment and dose escalation.

Detailed Explanation

GLP-1 receptor agonists are a class of medications that mimic the incretin hormone GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) to reduce appetite, lower blood sugar, and promote weight loss. The class includes semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy), tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound), liraglutide (Saxenda, Victoza), and dulaglutide (Trulicity), among others. Fatigue is a recognized side effect across the entire class, though rates vary by drug and dose.

Fatigue Rates by Medication

Semaglutide at the higher weight-management dose (Wegovy 2.4 mg) reports fatigue in approximately 11 percent of patients. At lower diabetes doses (Ozempic), rates are closer to 5 to 8 percent. Tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) reports fatigue in 5 to 9 percent of patients depending on dose. Liraglutide at the 3 mg weight-management dose (Saxenda) reports fatigue in roughly 5 to 7 percent. While the exact percentages vary, fatigue is consistently observed at rates higher than placebo across the class.

Why GLP-1 Medications Cause Fatigue

The primary mechanism is reduced caloric intake. All GLP-1 receptor agonists suppress appetite significantly, and the resulting caloric deficit forces the body to shift from dietary energy to stored fat for fuel. This metabolic transition takes several weeks, during which patients commonly feel tired. The more potent the appetite suppression (as seen with higher doses and dual-agonist medications like tirzepatide), the more likely fatigue is to occur.

Delayed gastric emptying, a hallmark effect of GLP-1 medications, also changes how nutrients are absorbed and can alter the timing of energy availability throughout the day. Patients may feel less energetic after meals than they did before treatment because food is being processed more slowly.

For patients with diabetes, GLP-1 medications lower blood sugar levels, and the transition to lower glucose can temporarily produce tiredness. Dehydration from GI side effects (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea) is another common and often underestimated contributor to fatigue.

Is Fatigue Temporary?

In most cases, yes. Fatigue associated with GLP-1 medications tends to peak during the first four to eight weeks of treatment and during each dose increase. Once patients stabilize on their maintenance dose and their body adapts to the new caloric intake and metabolic patterns, energy levels typically return to baseline or improve. Many patients ultimately report feeling more energetic after losing weight and improving their metabolic health.

When Fatigue Warrants Medical Attention

Mild fatigue during treatment initiation is expected and manageable. However, patients should contact their physician if fatigue is severe enough to interfere with work or daily activities, if it persists beyond two to three months at a stable dose, or if it is accompanied by other symptoms like dizziness, confusion, chest pain, or significant weakness. These could indicate dehydration, nutritional deficiency, anemia, thyroid dysfunction, or other conditions requiring separate evaluation.

What to Consider

  • Fatigue is a class-wide side effect of GLP-1 medications, not specific to any single drug.
  • Adequate protein intake (at least 60 grams daily, or as directed by your physician) helps preserve muscle mass and maintain energy during weight loss.
  • Consistent hydration is critical. Aim for at least 64 ounces of water daily.
  • Eat regular, balanced meals even when appetite is low. Skipping meals worsens fatigue.
  • Light exercise such as walking can improve energy levels and reduce feelings of tiredness.
  • Work with your prescriber to titrate doses gradually and extend time at a lower dose if side effects are significant.
  • Have routine blood work done to check for nutritional deficiencies, thyroid function, and blood counts if fatigue persists.
