What Is Semaglutide?

Semaglutide is a synthetic peptide that mimics a natural hormone in your body called glucagon-like peptide-1, or GLP-1. Your gut releases GLP-1 after you eat, and it plays several roles in metabolism, appetite regulation, and blood sugar control.

The medication is available under several brand names:

  • Wegovy (semaglutide 2.4 mg) is the FDA-approved version specifically for weight management
  • Ozempic (semaglutide 1.0 mg and 2.0 mg) is approved for type 2 diabetes but frequently prescribed off-label for weight loss
  • Rybelsus is an oral tablet form approved for diabetes
  • Compounded semaglutide is available from licensed compounding pharmacies, often at a significantly lower price

Both Wegovy and Ozempic are administered as once-weekly subcutaneous injections. You inject the medication just under the skin of your abdomen, thigh, or upper arm using a pre-filled pen or syringe. Most patients find the injection process straightforward and nearly painless.

How Does Semaglutide Work for Weight Loss?

Semaglutide produces weight loss through several interconnected mechanisms. Understanding how it works helps explain why it is so much more effective than previous weight loss medications.

Appetite Suppression

The primary way semaglutide drives weight loss is by reducing hunger. The medication crosses the blood-brain barrier and acts on GLP-1 receptors in the hypothalamus, which is the brain region that regulates appetite. Patients consistently report feeling less hungry throughout the day and feeling satisfied with smaller portions of food.

This is not a subtle effect. Most patients describe a dramatic shift in their relationship with food. Cravings diminish. The constant mental chatter about what to eat next quiets down. Many patients say it is the first time they have felt "normal" around food.

Delayed Gastric Emptying

Semaglutide slows down how quickly food leaves your stomach. This means you feel full longer after eating. It also contributes to some of the gastrointestinal side effects (more on that below), but the net result is that you naturally eat less because your body signals fullness sooner and for a longer period.

Improved Insulin Sensitivity

Semaglutide enhances your body's insulin response, particularly after meals. It stimulates insulin secretion in a glucose-dependent manner, meaning it helps your body process blood sugar more efficiently without causing dangerous drops in blood sugar. This improved metabolic function contributes to overall metabolic health beyond just weight loss.

Reduced Glucagon Secretion

The medication also lowers levels of glucagon, a hormone that raises blood sugar. By keeping glucagon in check, semaglutide helps your body maintain steadier blood sugar levels throughout the day. This reduces the spikes and crashes that often drive cravings and overeating.

STEP Trial Results: What the Research Shows

The evidence behind semaglutide for weight loss comes primarily from the STEP (Semaglutide Treatment Effect in People with Obesity) clinical trial program. These were large, rigorous, placebo-controlled studies that collectively enrolled thousands of participants.

STEP 1: The Landmark Study

STEP 1 enrolled 1,961 adults with obesity (BMI of 30 or higher) or overweight (BMI of 27 or higher) with at least one weight-related health condition. None of the participants had diabetes. All received lifestyle counseling alongside either semaglutide 2.4 mg weekly or placebo.

The results at 68 weeks were striking:

  • Semaglutide group lost an average of 14.9% of body weight compared to 2.4% in the placebo group
  • One-third of participants lost 20% or more of their body weight
  • 86.4% of semaglutide participants lost at least 5% of their body weight
  • 69.1% lost at least 10%
  • 50.5% lost at least 15%

To put those numbers in perspective, losing 5 to 10% of body weight is the threshold at which physicians see meaningful improvements in blood pressure, cholesterol, blood sugar, and other metabolic markers. Semaglutide helped the majority of participants exceed that threshold by a wide margin.

STEP 2: Patients with Type 2 Diabetes

STEP 2 focused on adults who had both obesity and type 2 diabetes. Participants lost an average of 9.6% of body weight on semaglutide 2.4 mg, along with significant improvements in blood sugar control. Weight loss tends to be slightly lower in patients with diabetes, which is a consistent finding across all GLP-1 studies.

STEP 3: Intensive Behavioral Therapy

STEP 3 combined semaglutide with intensive behavioral therapy, including a low-calorie diet for the first 8 weeks. Participants lost an average of 16% of body weight, showing that combining the medication with structured lifestyle changes can produce even greater results.

STEP 4: What Happens When You Stop

STEP 4 addressed one of the most important questions: what happens when you stop taking semaglutide? After 20 weeks on the medication, participants were randomized to either continue semaglutide or switch to placebo. Those who continued lost an additional 7.9% of body weight over the next 48 weeks. Those who switched to placebo regained about two-thirds of the weight they had lost.

This study made clear that semaglutide works best as an ongoing treatment, similar to medications for blood pressure or cholesterol. Obesity is a chronic condition, and the most successful outcomes come from sustained treatment.

STEP 5: Long-Term Data

STEP 5 followed participants for two full years. Those on semaglutide maintained an average weight loss of 15.2% at 104 weeks, demonstrating that the medication's effects are durable with continued use.

Semaglutide Dosing: The Titration Schedule

Semaglutide is not started at the full dose. Instead, physicians use a gradual dose escalation (titration) to minimize side effects and allow your body to adjust. The standard Wegovy titration schedule is:

Weeks Dose Purpose
Weeks 1-4 0.25 mg weekly Initial tolerability
Weeks 5-8 0.5 mg weekly Gradual increase
Weeks 9-12 1.0 mg weekly Intermediate dose
Weeks 13-16 1.7 mg weekly Approaching target
Week 17 onward 2.4 mg weekly Maintenance dose

This 16-week titration is designed to help your gastrointestinal system adjust gradually. Most side effects occur during dose increases and tend to improve as your body adapts.

Your prescribing physician may adjust this schedule based on how you respond. Some patients do well at lower doses and may not need to reach 2.4 mg to see meaningful results. Others may need to stay at a given dose level for longer before moving up if they experience significant side effects.

Injection Technique

Semaglutide is injected once weekly on the same day each week. You can choose any day that works for your schedule. The injection sites include:

  • Abdomen (at least 2 inches from the navel)
  • Front of the thigh
  • Upper arm

Rotate injection sites each week to avoid irritation. The needle is very fine, and most patients describe the injection as a brief pinch or say they barely feel it at all.

Semaglutide Side Effects

Like all medications, semaglutide has side effects. The most common are gastrointestinal and tend to be most pronounced during dose escalation.

Common Side Effects

  • Nausea (reported by approximately 44% of patients in clinical trials)
  • Diarrhea (approximately 30%)
  • Vomiting (approximately 24%)
  • Constipation (approximately 24%)
  • Abdominal pain (approximately 20%)
  • Headache
  • Fatigue
  • Injection site reactions

Most gastrointestinal side effects are mild to moderate, peak during dose increases, and improve over time. In STEP trials, only about 7% of participants discontinued treatment due to side effects.

Serious Side Effects (Rare)

  • Pancreatitis: Inflammation of the pancreas. Seek immediate medical attention for severe, persistent abdominal pain.
  • Gallbladder problems: Rapid weight loss can increase the risk of gallstones. Symptoms include pain in the upper right abdomen.
  • Thyroid tumors: In animal studies, semaglutide caused thyroid C-cell tumors in rodents. This has not been confirmed in humans, but the medication carries a boxed warning and is contraindicated in patients with a personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2 (MEN 2).
  • Kidney problems: Dehydration from vomiting or diarrhea can affect kidney function.
  • Allergic reactions: Rare but possible.

Managing Side Effects

We counsel all of our patients on practical strategies to minimize discomfort:

  • Eat smaller, more frequent meals instead of large portions
  • Avoid high-fat, greasy, or very rich foods, especially during titration
  • Stay well hydrated
  • Eat slowly and stop when you feel satisfied, not stuffed
  • Take your injection in the evening so that any nausea peaks while you sleep
  • Use ginger tea or over-the-counter remedies for mild nausea

If side effects become severe or do not improve, your physician may slow down the titration or adjust your dose.

Who Qualifies for Semaglutide?

The FDA-approved criteria for Wegovy (semaglutide for weight management) are:

  • BMI of 30 or higher (obesity), or
  • BMI of 27 or higher (overweight) with at least one weight-related health condition such as high blood pressure, type 2 diabetes, high cholesterol, or obstructive sleep apnea

Semaglutide is intended for adults aged 18 and older. It is also approved for adolescents aged 12 and older in certain cases.

Who Should Not Take Semaglutide

  • Patients with a personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma
  • Patients with Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2 (MEN 2)
  • Patients with a known hypersensitivity to semaglutide or any component of the formulation
  • Pregnant or breastfeeding women (semaglutide should be discontinued at least 2 months before a planned pregnancy)
  • Patients with a history of pancreatitis should discuss risks with their physician

Semaglutide vs. Other Weight Loss Options

Semaglutide vs. Tirzepatide

Tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) is a newer dual-agonist that targets both GLP-1 and GIP receptors. Head-to-head data suggests tirzepatide may produce slightly greater weight loss on average (up to 22.5% in clinical trials vs. about 15% for semaglutide). However, both are highly effective, and individual responses vary. Some patients do better on semaglutide, others on tirzepatide.

Semaglutide vs. Liraglutide

Liraglutide (Saxenda) is an older GLP-1 agonist that requires daily injections and produces about 5 to 8% average weight loss. Semaglutide is more effective, more convenient (weekly vs. daily), and has largely replaced liraglutide as the first-line GLP-1 option.

Semaglutide vs. Diet and Exercise Alone

The STEP trials included lifestyle counseling for all participants, including the placebo group. The placebo group (diet and exercise only) lost about 2.4% of body weight. The semaglutide group lost 14.9%. Semaglutide is not a replacement for healthy habits, but it addresses the biological barriers that make sustained weight loss so difficult for people with obesity.

Semaglutide vs. Bariatric Surgery

Bariatric surgery (gastric bypass, sleeve gastrectomy) typically produces 25 to 35% weight loss and remains the most effective intervention for severe obesity. However, it is an invasive surgical procedure with its own risks, recovery time, and lifestyle requirements. Semaglutide offers a non-surgical alternative that produces meaningful weight loss for many patients who either do not qualify for surgery, do not want surgery, or want to try medical management first.

Lifestyle Factors That Maximize Results

Semaglutide works best when combined with healthy lifestyle changes. Here is what we recommend to our patients:

Nutrition

  • Prioritize protein at every meal (aim for 25 to 30 grams per meal). Adequate protein intake helps preserve lean muscle mass during weight loss.
  • Focus on whole, nutrient-dense foods: vegetables, fruits, lean proteins, whole grains, and healthy fats.
  • Eat slowly and mindfully. Semaglutide makes it easier to recognize fullness signals, so pay attention to them.
  • Stay hydrated. Aim for at least 64 ounces of water daily.

Exercise

  • Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity per week (walking, cycling, swimming).
  • Include resistance training 2 to 3 times per week. This is especially important for preserving muscle mass during rapid weight loss.
  • Start where you are. If you are not currently active, begin with short daily walks and build from there.

Sleep and Stress

  • Aim for 7 to 9 hours of quality sleep per night. Poor sleep disrupts hunger hormones and makes weight loss harder.
  • Manage stress through whatever works for you: exercise, meditation, time outdoors, social connection.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I need to take semaglutide?

Obesity is a chronic condition, and semaglutide is most effective as an ongoing treatment. Most patients who stop the medication regain a significant portion of lost weight. Your physician will work with you to determine the best long-term plan, which may include a reduced maintenance dose.

Will I regain weight if I stop?

Data from STEP 4 shows that most patients regain weight after discontinuing semaglutide. This is not a failure of willpower. It reflects the biological reality that the hormonal and neurological factors driving weight regain are powerful. Ongoing treatment helps maintain the metabolic benefits.

Can I drink alcohol while taking semaglutide?

Moderate alcohol consumption is generally considered acceptable, but many patients find their tolerance decreases. Alcohol is also calorie-dense and can stimulate appetite. We recommend limiting alcohol intake, especially during the active weight loss phase.

Does semaglutide cause muscle loss?

Any significant weight loss involves some loss of lean mass along with fat. Studies suggest that about 25 to 40% of weight lost on semaglutide may be lean mass. This is why we strongly emphasize protein intake and resistance training for all of our patients.

Is compounded semaglutide safe?

Compounded semaglutide from a licensed, reputable compounding pharmacy contains the same active ingredient as brand-name products. The FDA has allowed compounding of semaglutide during shortage periods. We work exclusively with licensed pharmacies that follow strict quality and purity standards.

How soon will I see results?

Most patients notice reduced appetite within the first 1 to 2 weeks. Visible weight loss typically becomes apparent by weeks 4 to 8. Significant results (10% or more body weight loss) generally take 4 to 6 months.
