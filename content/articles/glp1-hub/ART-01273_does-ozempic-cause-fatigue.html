Does Ozempic Cause Fatigue?

Ozempic can cause fatigue in some patients. Tiredness is listed as a known side effect in the prescribing information, reported by approximately 5 to 8 percent of clinical trial participants. It is most common during the first weeks of treatment and when the dose is increased, and it usually resolves as the body adjusts.

Detailed Explanation

Ozempic is the brand name for semaglutide prescribed for type 2 diabetes management. It is administered as a once-weekly subcutaneous injection at doses of 0.25 mg, 0.5 mg, 1 mg, or 2 mg. While Ozempic is primarily approved for blood sugar control, many patients experience weight loss as well, and the medication's impact on appetite and metabolism can contribute to feelings of fatigue.

Clinical Evidence for Fatigue

In the SUSTAIN clinical trial program, which tested Ozempic across multiple studies involving thousands of patients with type 2 diabetes, fatigue was reported more frequently in the semaglutide groups than in placebo or active comparator groups. The FDA prescribing label for Ozempic lists fatigue among adverse reactions observed in clinical trials. While not among the top five most common side effects (which are predominantly GI-related), it affects a meaningful number of patients.

Why Ozempic Causes Fatigue

Several factors explain why patients may feel tired on Ozempic. Reduced food intake is the most common contributor. Ozempic significantly suppresses appetite, and when caloric intake drops suddenly, the body needs time to adjust its energy sources. This transition period can produce persistent low-grade tiredness.

For patients with type 2 diabetes, Ozempic's blood sugar-lowering effects can also play a role. While true hypoglycemia is uncommon with Ozempic alone, patients whose blood sugar was previously running high may feel tired as their levels normalize. The body has been functioning at elevated glucose levels, and the shift to healthier ranges can temporarily feel like an energy drop.

Dehydration is another factor. Nausea and decreased thirst awareness (from eating less) can lead to inadequate fluid intake. Even mild dehydration causes fatigue, reduced concentration, and general malaise.

Fatigue Timeline

Most patients notice fatigue during the 0.25 mg introductory phase and again when stepping up to 0.5 mg or 1 mg. The first two to four weeks at each new dose tend to be the hardest. By the time a patient has been on their maintenance dose for four to six weeks, fatigue typically diminishes significantly. Patients who continue to experience fatigue beyond three months should consult their physician for further evaluation.

What to Consider

  • Do not skip meals even if your appetite is low. Small, frequent, nutrient-dense meals help maintain energy.
  • Monitor your blood sugar as directed by your physician, especially if you take other diabetes medications alongside Ozempic.
  • Stay hydrated by drinking water consistently throughout the day.
  • Ensure adequate sleep, as appetite changes and nausea can disrupt sleep patterns.
  • Talk to your doctor if fatigue does not improve after several weeks at a stable dose or if it interferes with daily activities.
  • Nutritional deficiencies in iron, vitamin B12, or vitamin D can worsen fatigue and should be evaluated with blood work.
