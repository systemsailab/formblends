What Is Peptide Therapy?

Peptides are short chains of amino acids that act as signaling molecules in the body. Your body produces thousands of them naturally. They regulate hunger, metabolism, growth, immune function, and more. Peptide therapy involves using synthetic or bioidentical versions of specific peptides to enhance functions your body may not be performing optimally.

In the context of weight loss, the most clinically validated peptides are GLP-1 receptor agonists:

  • Semaglutide: A synthetic version of the GLP-1 hormone. Reduces hunger, slows digestion, and improves blood sugar control. Produces approximately 15% body weight loss. Semaglutide
  • Tirzepatide: A dual-action peptide that targets both GIP and GLP-1 receptors. Produces approximately 20-22% body weight loss, the highest of any current treatment. Tirzepatide

These are not fringe supplements. They are FDA-approved medications backed by large-scale clinical trials involving tens of thousands of participants.

Safety and Side Effects

What to Expect

The most common side effects of GLP-1 peptide therapy are gastrointestinal: nausea, constipation, diarrhea, and occasional vomiting. These are most pronounced during dose escalation (the first three to four months) and typically improve as your body adjusts.

Parenting Through Side Effects

The practical question every parent asks: "Will I be too sick to function?" For the vast majority of people, no. Side effects are mild to moderate and do not prevent normal activity. On days when nausea is worse, lean on easy meals for the family (sandwiches, cereal for dinner, or takeout) and stay hydrated. It passes.

Pregnancy and Breastfeeding

GLP-1 peptide medications must not be used during pregnancy or while breastfeeding. Semaglutide should be discontinued at least two months before planned conception. Tirzepatide requires at least a one-month washout. Use effective contraception during treatment if pregnancy is possible.

Medication Safety Around Children

Injection pens should be stored in the refrigerator, out of reach of children. Used needles go in a sharps container, not the regular trash. Treat these medications with the same precautions you would use for any household medication. Lock them up if your children are old enough to be curious.

Contraindications

GLP-1 peptide therapy is not appropriate for individuals with a personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or multiple endocrine neoplasia type 2 (MEN 2). It is also contraindicated during pregnancy. A history of pancreatitis requires careful provider evaluation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is peptide therapy the same as taking hormones?

Peptides are not the same as traditional hormone replacement therapy. GLP-1 peptides mimic a specific gut hormone to reduce appetite. They do not affect sex hormones, thyroid hormones, or growth hormone levels in clinically meaningful ways at standard doses.

How is peptide therapy different from diet pills?

Traditional diet pills (stimulants like phentermine) work by revving up your nervous system. Peptide therapy works by mimicking natural hormones that regulate hunger and metabolism. The mechanism is fundamentally different, as are the results. Peptide therapy produces two to three times more weight loss than older diet pills and addresses the underlying biology of overeating.

Can I take peptide therapy alongside my prenatal vitamins?

If you are taking prenatal vitamins because you are planning a pregnancy, you should not start peptide therapy. These medications must be stopped well before conception. If you are taking prenatals simply for nutritional support (common among parents), discuss this with your provider. There is generally no interaction, but your provider should know everything you are taking.

Will peptide therapy make me too tired to parent?

The opposite is more common. After the initial adjustment period, most patients report improved energy from stable blood sugar, better sleep, and reduced inflammation associated with carrying excess weight. You may find yourself saying yes to bike rides and playground trips more often.

How do I store the medication with kids in the house?

Keep injection pens in the refrigerator on a high shelf or in a container children cannot open. Used needles go in a puncture-proof sharps container. Many pharmacies provide these for free. Never leave pens or needles where children can access them.

What if my partner wants to start too?

Many couples begin peptide therapy together. Having a partner on the same treatment can make the process easier since you share similar appetite levels, food preferences shift together, and you can support each other through the adjustment period. Each person needs their own prescription and provider evaluation.
