Side Effects and Medical Screening

The purpose of physician screening before prescribing is to identify patients who may be at risk for serious side effects. Key contraindications include:

  • Personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma: Semaglutide carries a boxed warning due to thyroid C-cell tumor findings in animal studies. {{MEDICAL_REVIEW:MTC contraindication}}
  • Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2 (MEN 2): Absolute contraindication.
  • History of pancreatitis: Increased risk requires careful evaluation.
  • Pregnancy or breastfeeding: Semaglutide should be stopped at least 2 months before a planned pregnancy.
  • Severe kidney disease: Dehydration from GI side effects can worsen renal function.

What a Legitimate Online Program Looks Like vs. a Red Flag

The growth of telehealth weight loss has brought both reputable providers and questionable operators into the market. Knowing the difference protects your health and your wallet.

Signs of a Legitimate Program

  • Licensed physicians review every application individually. The provider can tell you which physician reviewed your case and what their credentials are.
  • Not everyone is approved. Programs that approve 100% of applicants are likely rubber-stamping prescriptions without genuine medical evaluation.
  • The pharmacy is named and verifiable. You should be able to look up the compounding pharmacy's license with your state board of pharmacy or the FDA's 503B outsourcing facility database.
  • Follow-up care is included and accessible. You should be able to reach a medical professional for questions about side effects, dose changes, or concerns.
  • Transparent pricing. All costs are disclosed upfront, including medication, consultation, shipping, and supplies.
  • Proper cold-chain shipping. Semaglutide must be shipped cold to maintain stability. Providers should use insulated packaging with cold packs.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • No physician interaction at all: Just a form and a credit card charge. This may violate state prescribing regulations.
  • Extremely low prices ($50 to $100/month): Licensed compounding of semaglutide has real costs. Prices this low may indicate questionable sourcing or unlicensed operations. {{MEDICAL_REVIEW:compounding cost floor analysis}}
  • Required supplement bundles: Some providers require you to purchase $100+ in supplements alongside the medication. This is a revenue tactic, not medical necessity.
  • No way to contact a doctor: If the only communication channel is email with multi-day response times, you may not have adequate medical oversight.
  • Guaranteed approval before any medical information is collected: No responsible physician guarantees a prescription before reviewing your health history.

State-by-State Considerations

While telehealth is legal across all 50 states, regulations vary in ways that can affect your experience:

  • Prescriber licensing: The physician prescribing your medication must hold an active license in the state where you reside at the time of the consultation. Reputable telehealth platforms maintain a network of physicians covering all states they serve.
  • Pharmacy shipping: The compounding pharmacy must be licensed to ship to your state. Most 503B outsourcing facilities hold licenses in all 50 states, but 503A pharmacies may have more limited shipping capabilities.
  • Controlled substance restrictions: Semaglutide is not a controlled substance, so the stricter telehealth rules around controlled substances (like the Ryan Haight Act's in-person evaluation requirement for Schedule II-V drugs) do not apply. This makes the online prescription process simpler for semaglutide than for medications like phentermine. {{CITE:telehealth prescribing by state 2026}}
