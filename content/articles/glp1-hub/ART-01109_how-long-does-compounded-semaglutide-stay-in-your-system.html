How Long Does Compounded Semaglutide Stay In Your System?

Compounded semaglutide stays in your system for approximately five weeks (35 days) after your last injection, the same duration as brand-name semaglutide products like Ozempic and Wegovy. The semaglutide molecule has a half-life of about seven days regardless of whether it comes from a manufacturer or a compounding pharmacy.

Why Compounded Semaglutide Has the Same Half-Life

The half-life of a medication is determined by the molecule itself, not by who manufactured it or how it was formulated. Compounded semaglutide uses the same active pharmaceutical ingredient as Ozempic and Wegovy. Once injected, it follows the same pharmacokinetic pathway: absorption from subcutaneous tissue, albumin binding in the bloodstream, slow proteolytic metabolism, and excretion through urine and feces over approximately five half-lives.

The elimination timeline after your last compounded semaglutide injection mirrors that of brand-name products:

  • Week 1: About 50% of the drug remains active in your system.
  • Week 2: About 25% remains.
  • Week 3: About 12.5% remains.
  • Week 4: About 6% remains.
  • Week 5: Less than 3% remains. The drug is effectively cleared.

What Makes Compounded Semaglutide Different

While the active molecule and its half-life are the same, compounded semaglutide formulations can differ from brand-name products in several ways that are worth understanding.

Formulation and excipients. Compounding pharmacies may use different inactive ingredients (buffers, preservatives, stabilizers) than those found in Ozempic or Wegovy. These differences do not change how long semaglutide stays in your system, but they can affect injection site reactions, stability, and comfort at the injection site.

Semaglutide salt forms. Some compounded products use semaglutide sodium or semaglutide acetate rather than the base form used in brand-name products. The salt form can influence the initial absorption rate and bioavailability, but the semaglutide molecule itself behaves identically once it enters the bloodstream. The half-life and elimination timeline remain the same.

Concentration and dosing. Compounded semaglutide is available in various concentrations, and dosing may be measured differently than brand-name pens. However, the principle remains: the half-life is determined by the molecule, not the dose. Whether you inject 0.25 mg or 2.5 mg, the rate of elimination stays consistent. Higher doses simply mean more total drug to clear at the same rate.

Sublingual formulations. Some compounding pharmacies offer sublingual semaglutide (dissolved under the tongue) rather than injectable. While the route of administration affects absorption and bioavailability, once semaglutide reaches the bloodstream, its half-life and elimination behave the same as the injectable form.

Quality Considerations That Can Affect Your Experience

Although the clearance timeline is the same, the quality of compounded semaglutide can vary between pharmacies. When semaglutide is sourced from a reputable 503B outsourcing facility that operates under FDA oversight, you can expect consistent potency and purity. Products from less regulated sources may have variable actual doses, which could affect your clinical experience even though the pharmacokinetics of whatever semaglutide is present remain unchanged.

This is why physician supervision matters. A prescribing physician who works with verified compounding pharmacies ensures that the semaglutide you receive is properly dosed, stored, and administered. This directly affects how predictable your treatment experience will be, even though the half-life of the molecule is inherent and unchanging.

What to Consider

If you are transitioning between compounded semaglutide and a brand-name product (or vice versa), the clearance timeline is the same, so there is no pharmacokinetic reason to wait longer or shorter for a switch. Your physician may adjust your dose during the transition to account for potential differences in bioavailability between formulations, but the five-week clearance window applies equally.

If you are stopping compounded semaglutide entirely, expect the same timeline of effects wearing off: appetite returning within one to two weeks, full return of pre-treatment hunger by four to six weeks, and the same considerations around surgery, pregnancy planning, and medication transitions that apply to any semaglutide product.
