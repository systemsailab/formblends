Brand-Name Semaglutide: Retail Pricing

Let's start with the sticker prices. These are the list prices for brand-name semaglutide without any insurance or discount programs.

Wegovy (Semaglutide for Weight Loss)

Wegovy is the FDA-approved semaglutide product specifically indicated for chronic weight management. It is manufactured by Novo Nordisk and available in five dose strengths as part of the titration schedule (0.25 mg, 0.5 mg, 1.0 mg, 1.7 mg, and 2.4 mg).

  • List price: Approximately $1,349 per month (four weekly doses)
  • Annual cost at list price: Approximately $16,188

This is the cash price you would pay at a retail pharmacy without any insurance coverage or savings card. Some pharmacies charge slightly more or less, but $1,349 is the standard benchmark.

Ozempic (Semaglutide for Diabetes, Used Off-Label)

Ozempic is approved for type 2 diabetes management. Many physicians prescribe it off-label for weight loss, though the maximum dose (2.0 mg) is lower than Wegovy's weight management dose (2.4 mg).

  • List price: Approximately $900 to $1,200 per month, depending on dose and pharmacy
  • Annual cost at list price: $10,800 to $14,400

Ozempic is sometimes slightly cheaper than Wegovy, but the difference is not dramatic when paying out of pocket. The price also varies depending on whether you need the 1.0 mg or 2.0 mg dose.

Rybelsus (Oral Semaglutide)

Rybelsus is the oral tablet form of semaglutide, approved for type 2 diabetes. It is sometimes used off-label for weight loss, though the available doses (7 mg and 14 mg daily) produce less weight loss than the injectable forms.

  • List price: Approximately $900 to $1,000 per month

Rybelsus is generally not the first choice for weight loss due to lower efficacy compared to injectable semaglutide, but some patients prefer a daily pill over a weekly injection.

Compounded vs. Brand: What's the Difference?

The active ingredient is the same: semaglutide. The differences are in manufacturing scale and regulatory pathway. Brand-name products go through FDA approval with large clinical trials. Compounded semaglutide may come in slightly different formulations. Some pharmacies add vitamin B12 or other ingredients. The concentration and injection volume may differ from brand-name pens. But the core medication doing the work is the same molecule.

Insurance Coverage for Semaglutide

Even though this guide focuses on out-of-pocket costs, it is worth understanding the insurance landscape because it affects your options.

Private Insurance (Employer-Sponsored Plans)

Coverage for anti-obesity medications has improved but remains inconsistent. Some large employers now cover Wegovy, especially those that have recognized the ROI of treating obesity (reduced healthcare costs, fewer sick days, improved productivity). However, many plans still classify weight loss medications as "lifestyle" drugs and exclude them.

If your plan does cover Wegovy, expect:

  • Prior authorization requirements (your doctor must demonstrate medical necessity)
  • Step therapy (you may need to try other approaches first)
  • Copays ranging from $25 to $150 per month with coverage
  • Possible quantity limits or time restrictions

Medicare

As of early 2026, Medicare does not cover weight loss medications. The Treat and Reduce Obesity Act has been introduced in Congress multiple times to change this, and the political momentum is growing, but no legislation has passed yet. If you are on Medicare, your best option for affordable semaglutide is compounded medication through a telehealth program.

Medicaid

Medicaid coverage varies dramatically by state. Some states cover Wegovy for qualifying patients, while others do not cover any anti-obesity medications. Check with your state Medicaid office or your managed care plan for specifics.

Marketplace Plans (ACA)

ACA marketplace plans vary by insurer and metal tier. Some Silver and Gold plans include weight loss medication coverage, but it is not guaranteed. Review your plan's formulary carefully before assuming coverage.

Savings Programs and Discounts

Novo Nordisk Savings Card (Wegovy)

Novo Nordisk offers a manufacturer savings card for Wegovy that can reduce your copay. Key details:

  • Available to patients with commercial insurance (not Medicare, Medicaid, or government insurance)
  • Can reduce your copay to as low as $0 for up to a specified number of fills
  • Does not apply to patients paying full cash price without insurance
  • Must be re-enrolled periodically

The savings card is helpful if you have insurance that covers Wegovy but leaves you with a high copay. It does not help uninsured patients.

Novo Nordisk Patient Assistance Program

For uninsured patients who meet income requirements, Novo Nordisk offers a patient assistance program (PAP) that provides Wegovy at no cost. Eligibility typically requires:

  • No health insurance coverage for Wegovy
  • Household income below a specified threshold (typically 400% of the federal poverty level)
  • U.S. residency

The application process involves paperwork and income verification. Approval can take several weeks. Your prescribing physician's office typically helps with the application.

GoodRx and Prescription Discount Cards

GoodRx and similar discount platforms can sometimes reduce the price of brand-name semaglutide at retail pharmacies. However, the discounts on semaglutide are typically modest compared to the list price. You might save $50 to $200, but you are still paying close to $1,000 per month.

These discount cards work best for generic medications. Since there is no generic semaglutide yet, the savings are limited.

HSA and FSA Accounts

If you have a Health Savings Account (HSA) or Flexible Spending Account (FSA), semaglutide is generally an eligible expense. This means you can pay with pre-tax dollars, which effectively saves you your marginal tax rate on the cost of the medication.

For someone in the 24% tax bracket paying $300 per month for compounded semaglutide, using HSA/FSA funds saves about $72 per month, bringing the effective cost down to roughly $228.

Important considerations:

  • You need a prescription from a licensed physician for the expense to be eligible
  • FSA funds expire at the end of the plan year (with some limited rollover), so plan accordingly
  • HSA funds roll over indefinitely and can be a smart way to pay for ongoing treatment

Why Is Brand-Name Semaglutide So Expensive?

Understanding why the price is so high helps explain the options available to you. Several factors drive the cost:

Research and Development

Novo Nordisk invested billions in developing semaglutide, running clinical trials, and navigating the FDA approval process. The STEP trial program alone enrolled thousands of patients across multiple countries. Pharmaceutical companies price drugs to recoup these investments and fund future research.

No Generic Competition (Yet)

Semaglutide is protected by patents, and no generic version is available. Without competition, Novo Nordisk has significant pricing power. When patents eventually expire, generic competition will likely bring prices down substantially, as it has with other medications. But that is still years away.

Demand Outpacing Supply

The demand for semaglutide has been extraordinary. Novo Nordisk has struggled to keep up with manufacturing, leading to intermittent shortages. High demand with constrained supply supports premium pricing.

The U.S. Pricing System

The United States does not regulate drug prices the way most other developed countries do. In countries with price negotiation or reference pricing, semaglutide costs significantly less. The lack of U.S. pricing controls allows manufacturers to set whatever price the market will bear.
